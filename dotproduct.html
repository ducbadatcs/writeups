The dot product in R n \mathbb{R}^n Rn , geometrically explained

Throughout this article, I will use the notation [ a ⃗ , b ⃗ ] [\vec{a}, \vec{b}] [a,b] to denote the notion "angle between a ⃗ \vec{a} a and b ⃗ \vec{b} b"

Definition

As you know, algebraically, in R n \mathbb{R}^n Rn, the dot product looks something like this:

a ⃗ ⋅ b ⃗ = ∑ i = 1 n a i b i \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i a⋅b=i=1∑n​ai​bi​

with a 1 , … , a n , b 1 , … , b n a_1, \dots, a_n, b_1, \dots, b_n a1​,…,an​,b1​,…,bn​ being “components” of the two vectors. But, this formula alone is kind of meaningless. Sure, you can use it to calculate the cosine of the angle between two vectors:

cos ⁡ ( [ a ⃗ , b ⃗ ] ) = a ⃗ ⋅ b ⃗ ∥ a ⃗ ∥ ∥ b ⃗ ∥ \cos ([\vec{a}, \vec{b}]) = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\|} cos([a,b])=∥a∥∥b∥a⋅b​

Now, strangely, I’ve never seen anyone asking why this is how it looks, and how does this value makes sense as a cosine (in the sense that its value lies between − 1 -1 −1 and 1 1 1)? Why are we dividing by the length of a ⃗ \vec{a} a and b ⃗ \vec{b} b divided? For the first equation, it can be established by the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality if you expand everything algebraically.

(and for a refresher, the length, also called the norm of a ⃗ \vec{a} a, and ∥ a ⃗ ∥ = ∑ i = 1 n a i 2 \displaystyle{\|\vec{a}\| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2}} ∥a∥=i=1∑n​ai2​​ )

. Sure, but why does the formula look like that? Why cosine anyway? Don’t worry, we’ll go slowly.

Angle between 2 vectors

Motivation

First, we’ll need to make sense on what an angle between two vectors even mean. One way you can consider it is that you draw them out, and use a measuring device. to calculate it. Sure, but you have two issues:

  1. It’s fine if two vectors make some nice angle with each other, like π 3 \frac{\pi}{3} 3π​ or something. But what if the angle is something a lot different? A weird property is that since angles are real numbers, most of them are transcendental, and that essentially means you have no really good way of writing them down without losing accuracy.
  2. How do you do that kind of measure in spaces with a lot of dimensions?

Because no matter what way you use to calculate the angle between two vectors, the consensus is that it is something akin to a relation between two vectors.

Now what do I mean by relation? The essence is simple, in this case, you will have two vectors and a certain value that says something about what they mean to each other. And that something here is the angle between those two, how much one vector would have to rotate until it hits the same direction as the other. For example, in the same vein as "you need to add 1 1 1 to 2 2 2 to get 3 3 3", we wish to say, "You rotate α \alpha α degrees from ( 0 , 1 ) (0, 1) (0,1) to get ( 1 , 0 ) (1, 0) (1,0)" (in this case α = 90 \alpha = 90 α=90). And if that doesn’t make sense, let’s try to have some examples.

Take two vectors. If they have the same direction, you’d expect their angle is 0 0 0 radians, and if they are opposites, the angle is π \pi π radians. Two perpendicular vectors should have an angle of π 2 \frac{\pi}{2} 2π​ radians. But the problems is that the possible angles are diverse, not every pair of vector is in the same direction, opposites, or perpendicular. We only really need these cases for now, and once we have a better notion of what [ a ⃗ , b ⃗ ] [\vec{a}, \vec{b}] [a,b] means, then we can go back two these examples and prove that they are indeed consistent with that.

We have a function here, so let’s consider its domain and range. For domain, it’s simply pairs of vectors in R n \mathbb{R}^n Rn. For range, it’s trickier. Well, normally, for an angle, we would like to admit a value in [ 0 , 2 π ) [0, 2\pi) [0,2π) or [ − π , π ] [-\pi, \pi] [−π,π] or even R \mathbb{R} R. However, the problem with angles is that if you add or remove 2 π 2\pi 2π from the angle, it remains functionally the same (of course not the exact same, but in all the elementary trigonometric functions, f ∈ { sin ⁡ , cos ⁡ , tan ⁡ , cot ⁡ , sec ⁡ , csc ⁡ } f \in \{\sin, \cos, \tan, \cot, \sec, \csc\} f∈{sin,cos,tan,cot,sec,csc}, they all satisfy f = f ( x ± k 2 π ) f = f(x \pm k2\pi) f=f(x±k2π) for integer k k k). Even then, the question on which interval range we should pick is tricky, should it be [ − π , π ) ) [-\pi, \pi)) [−π,π)) or [ 0 , 2 π ) [0, 2\pi) [0,2π) or something else entirely? Not to mention, it involves numbers like π \pi π, which is very complicated to deal with (it is one of those tricky transcendental numbers).

Well, all those angles we mentioned earlier are in radians, so what if we use degrees? Or even gradians? It doesn’t matter. Those units are basically convertible to each other anyway (since the way to convert between them are by basically multiplying with constants). And, no matter which one of them you use, they should satisfy the relation above? If a a a angle units are used to represent two perpendicular vectors, then 2 a 2a 2a is the angle between two opposite vectors. But that relation doesn’t have much value, since it’s still angles and we still are caught up in the problem of units above.

This is where sine and cosine come into play. They take an angle of any unit (but the default is radians, which we will use throughout this article) and turns it into a real number in the range [ − 1 , 1 ] [-1, 1] [−1,1], and if you want to get the angle back, there are inverses arcsin ⁡ \arcsin arcsin and arccos ⁡ \arccos arccos. For the sake of this article, we will not go too far in those inverse functions, but you can always assume that these functions will return their result in an interval of size 2 π 2\pi 2π radians.

Now, clearly [ − 1 , 1 ] [-1, 1] [−1,1] is a lot easier to work with (it is closed under multiplication, for instance), and it’s consistent between units. So instead of using the angle itself, we use either the sine or cosine of that angle, and go back to the angle via their inverses. But now, which one?

Let’s start with a little diagram.

A test of sin ⁡ \sin sin and cos ⁡ \cos cos

The art itself is in 2D space, but the idea remains universal. This four-arrow structure is easily constructible in all dimensions.

We have four vectors a ⃗ = O A undefined , b ⃗ = O B undefined , c ⃗ = O C undefined , d ⃗ = O D undefined \vec{a} = \overrightarrow{OA}, \vec{b} = \overrightarrow{OB}, \vec{c} = \overrightarrow{OC}, \vec{d} = \overrightarrow{OD} a=OA,b=OB,c=OC,d=OD, all with equal length and a ⃗ ⊥ b ⃗ , b ⃗ ⊥ c ⃗ , c ⃗ ⊥ d ⃗ \vec{a} \perp \vec{b}, \vec{b} \perp \vec{c}, \vec{c} \perp \vec{d} a⊥b,b⊥c,c⊥d which also has d ⃗ ⊥ a ⃗ \vec{d} \perp \vec{a} d⊥a; and a ⃗ = − c ⃗ \vec{a} = -\vec{c} a=−c and b ⃗ = − d ⃗ \vec{b} = -\vec{d} b=−d.

Let’s start by measuring the angle the vector a ⃗ \vec{a} a makes with itself, which is 0 0 0. Taking sin ⁡ ( 0 ) \sin(0) sin(0), we get 0 0 0, and taking cos ⁡ ( 0 ) \cos(0) cos(0), we get 1 1 1. What about a ⃗ \vec{a} a and c ⃗ \vec{c} c, two opposites? The angle is π \pi π, and sin ⁡ ( π ) = 0 \sin(\pi) = 0 sin(π)=0 with cos ⁡ ( π ) = − 1 \cos(\pi) = -1 cos(π)=−1. Now, this is where it starts getting interesting. The problem, is the sine function can’t be used to know if two vectors are pointing in the same or opposite direction, since its values on the angles referring to pointing the same and opposite direction is the same. This is in direct contrast to the cosine, which shows that if two vectors point in the same direction, and if they point in the opposite directions, then their cosine values are also direct opposites. One vote for cosine.

The other test is the perpendicular test. We have four vectors a ⃗ ⊥ b ⃗ ⊥ c ⃗ ⊥ d ⃗ \vec{a} \perp \vec{b} \perp \vec{c} \perp \vec{d} a⊥b⊥c⊥d, so their angles are either π 2 \frac{\pi}{2} 2π​ or − π 2 -\frac{\pi}{2} −2π​, depending on you look. And surprisingly, sin ⁡ \sin sin makes a difference here: sin ⁡ ( π 2 ) = 1 \sin \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 1 sin(2π​)=1 and sin ⁡ ( − π 2 ) = − 1 \sin \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -1 sin(−2π​)=−1 and cosine does not differentiate: cos ⁡ ( π 2 ) = cos ⁡ ( − π 2 ) = 0 \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \cos\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 0 cos(2π​)=cos(−2π​)=0 which means that for two perpendicular vectors, the sine of their angle depends on the individual direction they point to, while the cosine simply means 0 0 0. The split is actually important here, because if you work on lines instead of vectors, then lines’ have direction vectors that can point in two directions simultaneously; and any line perpendicular to the original line would be perpendicular to both of them; so it doesn’t matter what “type of perpendicular” you have to worry about when using cosine. So, another important vote for cosine.

Law of cosines

And that’s why we pick the cosine. In fact, another special relation is that the “closer” two vectors appear, the larger the value of the cosine of their angle is, a nice little treat. But that still doesn’t explain what cos ⁡ ( [ a ⃗ , b ⃗ ] ) \cos ([\vec{a}, \vec{b}]) cos([a,b]) means. And luckily, there IS a way to calculate the cosine of this angle, without even needing to calculate the angle itself. How? The law of cosines. But first, we need to make some claims.

First, the law of cosines only apply to two dimensions. Sure, you can go higher, but that means you have to consider if any set of three points forms a plane. But for all n n n, the answer is always true.

Lemma: Any set of three non-colinear points in R n , n ≥ 2 \mathbb{R}^n, n \geq 2 Rn,n≥2 belongs to a plane.

We have to define what a plane is in this one. Here, I’ll use the definition that a plane is the span of 2 linearly independent vectors and translated by some other vector (which can be zero, which means no translation). In other words, a plane is the set of points x = ( x 1 , x 2 , … , x n ) x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) x=(x1​,x2​,…,xn​) that satisfies:

[ x 1 x 2 ⋮ x n ] = [ e 1 e 2 ⋮ e n ] + t [ v 1 v 2 ⋮ v n ] + s [ w 1 w 2 ⋮ w n ] ∀ t , s ∈ R   ≡ x ⃗ = e ⃗ + t v ⃗ + s w ⃗ ,   ∀ t , s ∈ R \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}e_1 \\ e_2 \\ \vdots \\e_n\end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix}v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n\end{bmatrix} + s\begin{bmatrix}w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots \\w_n \end{bmatrix} \forall t, s \in \mathbb{R} \\~\\ \equiv \vec{x} = \vec{e} + t\vec{v} + s\vec{w}, \ \forall t, s \in \mathbb{R} ⎣⎡​x1​x2​⋮xn​​⎦⎤​=⎣⎡​e1​e2​⋮en​​⎦⎤​+t⎣⎡​v1​v2​⋮vn​​⎦⎤​+s⎣⎡​w1​w2​⋮wn​​⎦⎤​∀t,s∈R ≡x=e+tv+sw, ∀t,s∈R

With n = 1 n = 1 n=1 or n = 2 n = 2 n=2, the definition is that every point already belongs to the Euclidean line R 1 \mathbb{R}^1 R1 or the Euclidean plane E 2 \mathbb{E}^2 E2 (equivalent to R 2 \mathbb{R}^2 R2). With n ≥ 3 n \geq 3 n≥3, we note that if we define a plane as above, then for any three points A , B , C A, B, C A,B,C we substitute e ⃗ = A , v ⃗ = A B undefined , w ⃗ = A C undefined \vec{e} = A, \vec{v} = \overrightarrow{AB}, \vec{w} = \overrightarrow{AC} e=A,v=AB,w=AC (in coordinates), and it would satisfy the formula above.

With the plane present, we move on the law of cosines.
